The Citation Distortion Problem

“Prior research has established that Treatment X significantly reduces mortality in elderly patients.”

What the source actually said:
“Treatment X showed a trend toward reduced mortality in a small pilot cohort; larger randomized trials are needed.”

Citation distortion occurs when researchers misrepresent what a cited source actually found. Hedged findings become definitive claims. Pilot-study results are generalized to entire populations. Correlations are re-stated as causal mechanisms. These distortions propagate across the literature, compounding with each new paper that cites the distorting work.

A landmark study by Greenberg (2009, BMJ) traced a single fabricated Alzheimer’s claim through 242 papers over 15 years, finding the assertion grew stronger with each retelling — despite the original source having been retracted. Manual citation verification takes 15–30 minutes per claim. This system automates that process at scale.

Distortion Taxonomy

Five types of citation distortion detected by the system

Certainty Inflation

Hedging Erased

Hedging language is dropped; speculative findings are presented as established fact.

Citing paper: “Research has proven X causes Y.”
Source says: “Results suggest X may be associated with Y.”
Causal Overclaim

Correlation → Causation

Correlation is reported as causation; observational data cited as experimental proof.

Citing paper: “Exposure to X causes cognitive decline.”
Source says: “X was associated with cognitive decline in the cohort.”
Scope Inflation

Overgeneralization

Findings from a limited sample are generalized far beyond the study population.

Citing paper: “This effect holds across all adult populations.”
Source says: “Findings should not be generalized beyond this undergraduate sample.”
Cherry Picking

Selective Citation

Only the positive arm of a mixed-result study is cited; contradictory findings are omitted.

Citing paper: “Treatment Y significantly improved outcomes.”
Source says: “Treatment Y improved primary outcomes but showed no effect on secondary measures.”
Retracted Source

Post-Retraction Citation

The cited paper has since been formally retracted; citing it as evidence is invalid.

Citing paper: “As established by Chen et al. (2018)…”
Source says: “[RETRACTED — Data fabrication, March 2020]”
